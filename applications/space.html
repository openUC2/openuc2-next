Microscopy in orbit

Life sciences on orbital platforms — from ISS payloads to CubeSat biology experiments — demand microscopy hardware that survives launch vibration, radiation, thermal cycling from −20 °C to +60 °C in a vacuum, and then operates autonomously for months without a single hardware touch.

The space-qualified FRAME optical engine strips the cube platform to its essential inner core: a compact epi-fluorescence path, dual-color excitation, a through-objective IR autofocus module, and a small global-shutter CMOS — all hard-mounted in a conductive thermal chassis with conformal coating, thread-locker, and safety-wired connectors. The flight software implements a finite-state machine that boots, self-tests, calibrates, acquires, and reports health telemetry without ground intervention.

Design challenges & solutions

☢️ Radiation hardening

Latch-up protection and current limiting on all power rails. ECC-capable storage. Periodic memory scrubbing. Rad-tolerant or protected DC/DC converters.

🌡️ Thermal management

Conductive heat paths bonded to structural chassis. Athermal objective spacers. Conformal coating. Qualified across the full mission temperature profile.

🔒 Vibration qualification

Thread-locker on all fasteners. Safety-wired critical connectors. Foam/metal isolators per launch vibe profile. Hard-locked objective flange after on-ground calibration.

🤖 Autonomous operation

Finite-state machine: boot → self-test → calibrate → acquire → health-report. AF PID with fallback look-up tables. Watchdog restarts on any state-machine freeze.

📡 Telemetry & fault log

All acquisition events, hardware states, and faults logged to an isolated journaling partition. Telemetry frame transmitted on each ground pass.

🔭 Host robot interface

Isolated GPIO / TTL sync signals for XY positioning by host robot or robotic arm. No internal XY stage required — Z-focus only inside the engine.
